## Overview

This is the original contribution of this portfolio.

Projects 01–05 applied established international frameworks — the EU AI Act and NIST AI RMF — to Nigerian public sector AI systems. They demonstrated that global governance standards can and should be applied in African contexts.

This project asks a harder question: **are those frameworks sufficient for Sub-Saharan Africa?**

The EU AI Act was designed for European regulatory infrastructure. The NIST AI RMF was designed for US federal agencies and large private sector firms. Both assume institutional capacities — legal enforcement mechanisms, technical audit expertise, well-resourced compliance teams, and vendor disclosure obligations — that most African public institutions do not yet have.

Sub-Saharan African governments are procuring AI systems at scale — in healthcare, agriculture, education, social protection, financial inclusion, and electoral administration — without governance frameworks designed for their institutional realities. The result is a growing accountability gap: AI systems making consequential decisions about African citizens, with no structured mechanism for those citizens, institutions, or oversight bodies to understand, challenge, or hold those systems accountable.

This framework is designed to close that gap.

## Why This Framework Is Different

Most AI governance frameworks produced for African contexts are one of two things:

1. **Principles documents** — lists of values with no methodology for assessing whether those values are being honoured in practice
2. **Western framework summaries** — explanations of the EU AI Act or NIST AI RMF with a note that "African countries should consider adopting similar approaches"

This framework is neither. It is a **practical audit methodology** structured around ten accountability domains, a five-level maturity model, and an actionable scorecard calibrated to African institutional realities.

It directly addresses four governance challenges that Western frameworks do not adequately cover:
- What governance controls are feasible in low-resource institutional settings?
- How should donor-funded AI systems be audited when accountability is fragmented?
- What should auditors do when vendors refuse to disclose model details?
- How should institutions evaluate AI systems trained primarily on non-African datasets?

## Intended Users

- **Government ministries** procuring or deploying AI in public service delivery
- **Civil society organisations** monitoring AI deployments affecting their communities
- **International donors** embedding accountability conditions in AI-funded programmes
- **Regional bodies** (African Union, ECOWAS, EAC) developing continental AI governance standards
- **Researchers** studying AI governance in Global South contexts
